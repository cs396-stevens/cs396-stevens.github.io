This course presents the basic concepts of computer security, the different vulnerabilities that can occur throughout a system, how malicious attackers exploit these vulnerabilities, the defenses that can prevent or mitigate an attack, and the consequences and costs of attacks to individuals, organizations and societies. Topics include the security of cryptographic schemes, system software, networks, databases and programs, as well as the ethical, legal, and regulatory considerations surrounding data privacy and security.

Learning Objectives

  • (Cryptographic Systems) Compare and contrast private and public key cryptosystems, and identify the strengths and potential pitfalls of cryptographic systems. [Analysis]
  • (Systems Software) Illustrate and detect vulnerabilities in systems; explain how attacks can exploit such vulnerabilities; explain the defense mechanisms that can detect and prevent such attacks; and explain malware propagation mechanisms and defense strategies. [Development]
  • (Data privacy and security) Analyze access control mechanisms to restrict database access, and use cryptography to maintain the privacy of data stored in databases. [Professionalism]
  • (Network Security) Evaluate standard protocols to secure network communications and apply them in different scenarios; describe vulnerabilities in networking protocols that can be used to attack organizations; and understand how these can be detected or prevented. [Development]
  • (Programming for Security) Analyze how programs inadvertently create security vulnerabilities, and how these can be detected and prevented at the design, implementation, or deployment stage. [Development]
  • (Social Impact) Describe the implications of malicious attacks on computer systems to individuals, organizations, and society. [Professionalism]
  • (Ethics) Explain ethical issues in cybersecurity and state how the job of a typical IT professional might be connected to major technology-related ethical issues of the day. [Professionalism]

Format and Structure

Course attendance consists of:

  • 3 weekly ~1hr lectures (Mon, Wed, Fri)
  • 1 weekly ~2hr lab session (Thu)

Attendance is required for both lectures and lab sessions.

Lectures

Lecture attendance is tracked using in-class participation exercises on Poll Everywhere. These exercises are graded on completion, not correctness. If you complete 70% of these exercises for the semester, you will receive full credit for participation.

Labs

This semester we will be using pwn.college for most of our lab activities. In general, these will consist of a series of increasingly difficult capture-the-flag challenges related to the concepts covered in lectures.

Labs should be completed collaboratively, but everyone is responsible for completing exercises individually. Completion is tracked on pwn.college.

  • Labs are on Thursdays and attendance is required and will be checked at random throughout the semester
  • Labs will be released by Wednesday 12pm before lab sections (Thursday)
  • Labs will be due by Friday 11:59pm the following day
  • Each lab has a short post-lab survey that must be completed and submitted by the Friday deadline.

Challenge Clobbering

To incentivize you to spend extra time on some of the pwn.college modules we think will be helpful in this course, we’re introducing a lab challenge clobber policy.

For each module completed from the ones listed below, you can replace a single challenge/level from any lab.

  • (Linux Luminarium) Comprehending Commands
  • (Linux Luminarium) Digesting Documentation
  • (Linux Luminarium) Shell Variables
  • (Linux Luminarium) Practicing Piping
  • (Linux Luminarium) Chaining Commands

So for example, if I completed all of the “Comprehending Commands” module and could not figure out how to complete “Examining Memory” challenge from lab 2 before the deadline, my completion of “Comprehending Commands” would be used to clobber the “Examining Memory” challenge and I would still get credit for completion, as long as “Comprehending Commands” was completed before the lab 2 deadline.

Clobbering will be applied automatically.

Homework

There will be 5 or 6 problem set assignments throughout this course that may also include programming, and short essay components. These will be graded on correctness.

You may discuss these problem sets conceptually with other students but you may not directly share solutions or code.

All students are still responsible for submitting their solutions individually on Gradescope.

Late Policy

Students have 3 total slip days to use throughout the semester for extensions on homework. Slip days will be applied automatically.

Following any slip days used, there will be a 20% penalty for each additional day late, with a maximum penalty of 50%. The last day to submit all homeworks is 12/10. Please do not abuse this mercy.

In the case of medical emergencies or exceptionally extenuating circumstances, please contact the course instructor as soon as possible.

Academic Integrity

Generative AI

You may use generative AI technology, e.g. Chat-GPT, with care to generate ideas and brainstorm. However, you should note that the material generated by these programs may be inaccurate, incomplete, or otherwise problematic. You may treat these programs like a virtual fellow student: you are allowed to “discuss” with them at a conceptual level (though be aware that the information they provide may be inaccurate), but you cannot share solutions or code in either direction. Presenting AI-generated work as your own is plagiarism. If you include material generated by an AI program, it should be cited like any other reference material and clearly attributed. Any plagiarism or other form of cheating will be dealt with under relevant Stevens policies.

Disability Services Confidentiality Policy

Student Disability Files are kept separate from academic files and are stored in a secure location within the Office of Disability Services. The Family Educational Rights Privacy Act (FERPA, 20 U.S.C. 1232g; 34CFR, Part 99) regulates disclosure of disability documentation and records maintained by Stevens Disability Services. According to this act, prior written consent by the student is required before our Disability Services office may release disability documentation or records to anyone. An exception is made in unusual circumstances, such as the case of health and safety emergencies.
